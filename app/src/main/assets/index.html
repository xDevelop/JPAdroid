Educational App for Josephson Parametric Microwave Amplification

Abstract

Parametric amplification uses nonlinear variations in system parameters for amplification of a microwave signal. Josephson junctions are well suited as a non-linearity in a parametric amplifier, due to their nonlinear Josephson inductance. The noise added by a Josephson parametric amplifier (JPA) is quantum limited. Therefore, a quantum mechanical treatment of the amplifier device is necessary for low signal energies. The proposed app should give an overview of the classical, as well as the quantum mechanical treatment of the JPA. Starting from a simple circuit representation, the classical voltage-current relations in the circuit will be derived and solved for sinusoidal excitation. In parallel, the energy relations of the circuit will be calculated, in order to derive the Lagrangian of the system. The Hamilton function of the JPA is obtained by Legendre transform. Second quantization is used for deriving the quantum mechanical system Hamiltonian. In order to calculate the time evolution of the signal energy, we evaluate the Heisenberg equations of motion for the signal creation- and annihilation operators. The user of the proposed app should be able to vary all system parameters, like signal frequency, DC pump-voltage, maybe even losses, etc. The application should serve as an introduction to a classical as well as a quantum mechanical treatment of microwave amplification. Students using the app should get a deeper insight on the mechanisms involved in parametric amplification by varying system parameters, where the response of the system is immediately displayed.

Calculations

For a classical as well as a quantum mechanical treatment of the Josephson parametric amplifier (JPA) we consider the circuit representation given by the following figure.

The DC-pumped negative resistance JPA consists of a Josephson Junction, connected to two resonant circuits, one representing the actual signal, we want to amplify and one acting as an idler circuit, responsible for the second mode considered in the three-signal mixing non-degenerate case. The pump field is generated by the DC voltage across the Josephson Junction. In order to perform canonical quantization, we need to introduce canonic conjugate variables. Let us start with the first Josephson equation. It gives a relation between the super-current through the junction and the quantum phase difference on both sides of the barrier between the two superconductors,

$$ \begin{equation} \function{i_J}{t}=I_c\cdot\sin\left(\function{\phi}{t}\right)\, .\label{equation-first-josephson} \end{equation} $$

The second Josephson equation relates the quantum phase difference to the voltage across the junction,

$$ \begin{equation} \frac{\partial{\function{\phi}{t}}}{\partial{t}}=\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}\function{v_J}{t}\, .\label{equation-second-josephson} \end{equation} $$

Integrating equation (\ref{equation-second-josephson}) and inserting the result into (\ref{equation-first-josephson}) yields a relation for the Josephson current, depending on the voltage across the junction,

$$ \begin{equation} \function{i_J}{t}=I_c\cdot\sin\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}\int_{0}^{t}\,\function{v_J}{\tau}\,\diff{\tau}\right)\, . \end{equation} $$

The voltage in the signal circuit, consisting of $C_1$ and $L_1$ is equal to

$$ \begin{equation} \function{v_1}{t}=L_1\frac{\diff{\function{i_{L1}}{t}}}{\diff{t}}\, . \end{equation} $$

The current through the inductor $L_1$ is given by

$$ \begin{equation} \function{i_{L1}}{t}=\function{i_J}{t}-C_1\frac{\diff{\function{v_1}{t}}}{t}\, . \end{equation} $$

The same holds for the idler circuit, where the equations are obtained by changing indices from $1$ to $2$. Let us now perform a change of variables, where we express the voltage across the inductor in terms of the magnetic flux,

$$ \begin{equation} \function{\Phi_1}{t}=\int_{0}^{t}\,\function{v_1}{\tau}\,\diff{\tau}\, . \end{equation} $$

The dynamics of the signal circuit can be derived from following equation,

$$ \begin{equation} C_1\frac{\diff^2{\function{\Phi_1}{t}}}{\diff{t}^2}+\frac{1}{L}\function{\Phi_1}{t}=\function{i_J}{t}\, . \end{equation} $$

For quantum mechanics, we are interested in the total energy relation of the system. The magnetic energy stored in the inductor is equal to

$$\begin{equation} \function{W_{\mathrm{mag},1}}{t}=\frac{1}{2L_1}\function{\Phi_1^2}{t}\, . \end{equation} $$

The electric energy stored in the capacitor is given by

$$\begin{equation} \function{W_{\mathrm{el},1}}{t}=\frac{C_1}{2}\left(\frac{\diff{\function{\Phi_1}{t}}}{\diff{t}}\right)^2\, . \end{equation} $$

In this case, we can treat the electric energy $\function{W_{\mathrm{el},1}}{t}$ as some sort of kinetic energy, since it involves a derivative of the respective generalized variable $\function{\Phi_1}{t}$ and $\function{W_{\mathrm{mag},1}}{t}$ as a potential energy, since there are no temporal variations involved. The same holds for the idler circuit, with the energies, $\function{W_{\mathrm{el},2}}{t}$ and $\function{W_{\mathrm{mag},2}}{t}$, given by

$$ \begin{align} \function{W_{\mathrm{mag},2}}{t}&=\frac{1}{2L_2}\function{\Phi_2^2}{t}\, ,\\ \function{W_{\mathrm{el},2}}{t}&=\frac{C_2}{2}\left(\frac{\diff{\function{\Phi_2}{t}}}{\diff{t}}\right)^2\, . \end{align} $$

In general, the Lagrange function for a system with multiple variables is given by $\mathcal{L}=T-V$, where $T$ is the kinetic represents kinetic energy and $V$ represents potential energy.

The last step remaining, before we can formulate a Lagrange function for the complete JPA circuit is to derive the energy, stored in the Josephson junction itself, the so called Josephson energy. The energy in the Josephson junction is given by

$$ \begin{align} \function{E_J}{t}&=\int_{0}^{t}\,\function{v_j}{\tau}\function{i_J}{\tau}\,\diff{\tau}\nonumber\\ &=\frac{\hbar}{2e_0}\int_{0}^{t}\,\function{i_J}{\tau}\frac{\diff{\function{\phi}{\tau}}}{\diff{\tau}}\,\diff{\tau}\nonumber\\ &=\frac{\hbar I_c}{2e_0}\int_{0}^{\function{\phi}{t}}\sin\left(\function{\phi}{\tau}\right)\,\diff{\function{\phi}{\tau}}\nonumber\\ &=\frac{\hbar I_c}{2e_0}\left(1-\cos\left(\function{\phi}{t}\right)\right)\, , \end{align} $$

with

$$ \begin{align} \function{\phi}{t}&=\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}\int_{0}^{t}\,\function{v_J}{\tau}\,\diff{\tau}=\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\nonumber\\ &+\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}\left(\function{\Phi_1}{t}+\function{\Phi_2}{t}\right)\, . \end{align} $$

The Josephson energy also seems to be a potential energy, since there are no time derivatives involved. With this, we can write down the total Lagrange function $\function{\mathcal{L}}{\Phi_1,\Phi_2,\dot{\Phi}_1,\dot{\Phi}_2}$ of the JPA circuit

$$ \begin{align} \mathcal{L}&=\frac{C_1}{2}\dot{\Phi}_1^2+\frac{C_2}{2}\dot{\Phi}_2^2-\frac{1}{2L_1}\Phi_1^2\nonumber\\ &-\frac{1}{2L_2}\Phi_2^2-\frac{\hbar I_c}{2e_0}\nonumber\\ &+\frac{\hbar I_c}{2e_0}\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}\left[V_0t+\Phi_1+\Phi_2\right]\right). \end{align} $$

In order to derive the Hamilton function of the system, let us introduce the canonical conjugate momentum $Q_i$ to our system variable $\Phi_i$,

$$ \begin{equation} Q_i=\frac{\diff{\mathcal{L}}}{\diff{\dot{\Phi}_i}}\, . \end{equation} $$

Hence, we obtain

$$ \begin{align} Q_1&=C_1\dot{\Phi}_1\, ,\\ Q_2&=C_2\dot{\Phi}_2\, . \end{align} $$

The Hamilton function $\function{\mathcal{H}}{\Phi_1,\Phi_2,Q_1,Q_2}$ is obtained from the Lagrange function by Legendre transform, i.e.

$$ \begin{align} \mathcal{H}&=\frac{Q_1^2}{2C_1}+\frac{Q_2^2}{2C_2}+\frac{\Phi_1^2}{2L_1}\nonumber\\ &+\frac{\Phi_2^2}{2L_2}+\frac{\hbar I_c}{2e_0}\nonumber\\ &+\frac{\hbar I_c}{2e_0}\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}\left[V_0t+\Phi_1+\Phi_2\right]\right)\, . \label{equation-system-hamiltonian} \end{align} $$

For a later quantum mechanical treatment, we make a Taylor expansion of the cosine term in the Hamilton function above, i.e.

$$ \begin{align} &\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}\left[V_0t+\Phi_1+\Phi_2\right]\right)\nonumber\\ &=\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\right)\nonumber\\ &-\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}\sin\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\right)\left[\Phi_1+\Phi_2\right]\nonumber\\ &-\frac{2e_0^2}{\hbar^2}\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\right)\left[\Phi_1+\Phi_2\right]^2\nonumber\\ &+\function{\mathcal{O}}{\left[\Phi_1+\Phi_2\right]^3}\, . \label{equation-approx} \end{align} $$

We can now derive the classical equations of motion from the Hamiltonian by using

$$ \begin{align} \dot{\Phi}_i=\frac{\partial{\mathcal{H}}}{\partial{Q_i}}\, ,\\ \dot{Q}_i=-\frac{\partial{\mathcal{H}}}{\partial{\Phi_i}}\, . \end{align} $$

Thus we obtain a system of ordinary differential equations,

$$ \begin{align} \frac{\diff{Q_1}}{\diff{t}}&=-\frac{\Phi_1}{L_1}-I_c\sin\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\right)\nonumber\\ &-\frac{2e_0 I_c}{\hbar}\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\right)\Phi_1\nonumber\\ &-\frac{2e_0 I_c}{\hbar}\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\right)\Phi_2\, ,\\ \frac{\diff{Q_2}}{\diff{t}}&=-\frac{\Phi_2}{L_2}-I_c\sin\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\right)\nonumber\\ &-\frac{2e_0 I_c}{\hbar}\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\right)\Phi_1\nonumber\\ &-\frac{2e_0 I_c}{\hbar}\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}V_0t\right)\Phi_2\, ,\\ \frac{\diff{\Phi_1}}{\diff{t}}&=\frac{Q_1}{C_1}\, ,\\ \frac{\diff{\Phi_2}}{\diff{t}}&=\frac{Q_2}{C_2}\, . \end{align} $$

From now on, we can guess the quantum mechanical Hamiltonian $\operator{H}$ of the system by replacing the classical system variables $\Phi_1$, $\Phi_2$, $Q_1$, and $Q_2$ with quantum mechanical operators $\operator{\Phi_1}$, $\operator{\Phi_2}$, $\operator{Q_1}$, and $\operator{Q_2}$. The flux operators $\operator{\Phi_i}$ as generalized position operators and the charge operators $\operator{Q_i}$ as generalized momentum operators must fulfill the commutator relation

$$ \begin{equation} \commutator{\operator{\Phi_i}}{\operator{Q_j}}=\i\hbar\kronecker{ij}\, . \end{equation} $$

The resulting system Hamiltonian is

$$ \begin{align} \operator{H}&=\frac{1}{2C_1}\operator{Q_1}^2+\frac{1}{2C_2}\operator{Q_2}^2\nonumber\\ &+\frac{1}{2L_1}\operator{\Phi_1}^2+\frac{1}{2L_2}\operator{\Phi_2}^2\nonumber\\ &+\frac{\hbar I_c}{2e_0}\left(1-\cos\left(\frac{2e_0}{\hbar}\left[V_0t+\operator{\Phi_1}+\operator{\Phi_2}\right]\right)\right)\, .\label{equation-quantum-hamiltonian} \end{align} $$

Introducing the annihilation operators $\operator{a_i}$ and the creation operators $\operator{a_i}^\dagger$, given by

$$ \begin{align} \operator{a_i}&=\sqrt{\frac{1}{2\hbar\omega_i L_i}}\operator{\Phi_i}+\i\sqrt{\frac{\omega_i L_i}{2\hbar}}\operator{Q_i}\, ,\\ \operator{a_i}^\dagger&=\sqrt{\frac{1}{2\hbar\omega_i L_i}}\operator{\Phi_i}-\i\sqrt{\frac{\omega_i L_i}{2\hbar}}\operator{Q_i}\, . \end{align} $$ $$ \begin{align} \operator{\Phi_i}&=\sqrt{\frac{\hbar\omega_i L_i}{2}}\left(\operator{a_i}+\operator{a_i}^\dagger\right)\, ,\\ \operator{Q_i}&=\i\sqrt{\frac{\hbar}{2\omega_i L_i}}\left(\operator{a_i}-\operator{a_i}^\dagger\right)\, . \end{align} $$ $$ \begin{align} \operator{H_0}&=\frac{1}{2C_1}\operator{Q_1}^2+\frac{1}{2L_1}\operator{\Phi_1}^2+\frac{1}{2C_2}\operator{Q_2}^2+\frac{1}{2L_2}\operator{\Phi_2}^2\nonumber\\ &=\frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega_1\left(\operator{a_1}^\dagger\operator{a_1}+\operator{a_1}\operator{a_1}^\dagger\right)\nonumber\\ &+\frac{1}{2}\hbar\omega_2\left(\operator{a_2}^\dagger\operator{a_2}+\operator{a_2}\operator{a_2}^\dagger\right)\, . \end{align} $$ $$ \begin{equation} \operator{H_0}=\hbar\omega_1\left(\operator{a_1}^\dagger\operator{a_1}+\frac{1}{2}\right)+\hbar\omega_2\left(\operator{a_2}^\dagger\operator{a_2}+\frac{1}{2}\right)\, . \end{equation} $$

$$ \begin{align} \frac{\diff}{\diff{t}}\expectation{\operator{a_2}^\dagger\operator{a_2}}&=\frac{\i I_c c_2}{\hbar}\left(\expectation{\operator{a_2}}-\expectation{\operator{a_2}^\dagger}\right)\sin{\left(\omega_0 t\right)}\nonumber\\ &+\frac{2\i e_0 I_c c_2^2}{\hbar^2}\left(\expectation{\operator{a_2}\operator{a_2}}-\expectation{\operator{a_2}^\dagger\operator{a_2}^\dagger}\right)\cos{\left(\omega_0 t\right)}\nonumber\\ &+\frac{2\i e_0 I_c c_1 c_2}{\hbar^2}\left(\expectation{\operator{a_1}\operator{a_2}}-\expectation{\operator{a_1}\operator{a_2}^\dagger}\right.\nonumber\\ &+\left. \expectation{\operator{a_1}^\dagger\operator{a_2}}-\expectation{\operator{a_1}^\dagger\operator{a_2}^\dagger}\right)\cos{\left(\omega_0 t\right)}\, . \end{align} $$
